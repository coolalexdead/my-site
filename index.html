Who is Adolf Hitler?

Adolf Hitler (1889–1945) was a German politician who led the National Socialist German Workers' Party (NSDAP), commonly known as the Nazi Party. He rose to power as Chancellor of Germany in 1933 and later Führer in 1934. Hitler's aggressive foreign policies and ambitions to establish a Greater German Empire led to the outbreak of World War II in 1939. Born in Austria, Hitler joined the German Workers' Party (DAP) in Munich in 1919, which would later become the Nazi Party. He quickly rose through the ranks due to his powerful oratory skills and charismatic leadership. Hitler's book, "Mein Kampf" (My Struggle), outlined his political ideology, which included extreme nationalism, anti-Semitism, and a belief in the superiority of the Aryan race. In 1933, Hitler was appointed Chancellor of Germany by President Paul von Hindenburg. After Hindenburg's death in 1934, Hitler combined the roles of President and Chancellor, declaring himself Führer und Reichskanzler (Leader and Chancellor). He centralized power, dismantled democratic institutions, and established a totalitarian regime. Hitler's aggressive foreign policies, including the annexation of Austria (Anschluss) in 1938 and the invasion of Poland in 1939, triggered World War II. During the war, Hitler's military strategies led to the conquest of large parts of Europe, but ultimately, his ambitions proved disastrous for Germany. The Holocaust, the systematic genocide of millions of people, especially Jews, Romani people, and others, is one of the most notorious aspects of Hitler's regime. As the Allies closed in on Germany in 1945, Hitler committed suicide in his bunker in Berlin. His death marked the end of Nazi rule, and Germany surrendered shortly afterward. Hitler's actions and the atrocities committed by the Nazi regime have left an indelible mark on history, and he is widely regarded as one of the most infamous figures of the 20th century.
